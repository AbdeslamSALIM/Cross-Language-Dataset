In 1645 after the death of Krzysztof Wiesiołowski, the last of the clan, Białystok became the property of the Commonwealth.
Four years later, as a dowry of his daughter Aleksandra, it passed to the Branicki family.In the second half of the 18th century Field Crown Hetman Jan Klemens Branicki, a commander in chief, became the heir of the Białystok area.
Due to an industrial boom the population grew from 13,787 in 1857, and 56,629 in 1889, to 65,781 in 1901.
In this period the majority of the city's population was Jewish.After the outbreak of the First World War in 1914, the first heavy bombing of the town took place on 20 April 1915.
During 1920, when overrun by Soviet forces during the Polish-Soviet War, it briefly served as headquarters of the Polish Revolutionary Committee headed by Julian Marchlewski, which attempted to declare the Polish Soviet Socialist Republic.In the years 1920–1939 the city was again part of independent Poland.
